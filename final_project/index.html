This research project, led by Leah Rosenzweig (Development Innovation Lab) and Molly Offer-Westort (University of Chicago), is an example of efforts to leverage mobile technology to increase vaccinatination rates in Africa. The research uses an online experimental Facebook chatbot to study the effectiveness of different messaging strategies on increasing vaccination rates in Kenya and Nigeria. The study included a total of 22,014 social media users who were randomly assigned to one of three conditions. In the first condition, users engaged with a chatbot that elicited their concerns about the COVID-19 vaccine and provided targeted messaging in response to those concerns. In the second condition, users engaged with a chatbot that gave a general public service announcement (PSA) about the importance of the COVID-19 vaccine. In the third condition, users engaged with a chatbot to answer survey questions, with no additional information.

Despite Facebook's popularity, it's to be expected that research drawing on its user base is not going to entirely representative of the country as a whole. So, in an effort to better understand how representative the study sample is of the populations of both countries, I cleaned and extracted averages across 7 key descriptive statistics generated from an intake survey given to respondents in the study, then compared them to the same descriptive statistics pulled from the most recent round of the Afrobarometer survey. After running the intervention and surveying the respondents before and after regarding their vaccine willingness and intentions, researchers found that both the concern-addressing and PSA chatbots were effective at increasing vaccine willingness and intentions compared to the control condition, with the concern-addressing chatbot having a slightly greater effect. These findings suggest that using tailored, concern-based messaging may be a more effective approach than using generic PSAs to increase vaccine uptake in Africa.

To illustrate these effects, the two grouped bar charts below separate out the vaccine willingness and intentions of individuals before and after the intervention (as indicated by the legend on the right), by concern. As is shown below, in both the Nigeria and Kenys samples, concerns about (lack of) trust in government and side effects were the most amenable to treatment, since in both cases, the differences in means were respectively 99% (p<.01) and 99.9% (p<.001) likely due to the intervention and not chance.

Conclusion

The research conducted using online experimental Facebook chatbots found that, compared to a control group, both the PSA chatbot and the concern-addressing chatbot were able to increase vaccine willingness and intentions among social media users in Kenya and Nigeria. The PSA chatbot, which provided a general public service announcement about the importance of the COVID-19 vaccine, was slightly more effective than the control group in increasing these measures. However, the concern-addressing chatbot, which elicited specific concerns about the vaccine and provided tailored responses, was even more effective at increasing vaccine willingness and intentions. These results suggest that policymakers may wish to consider using communication techniques that address specific concerns and provide tailored messaging, rather than relying on generic public service announcements, in order to increase COVID-19 vaccine uptake. This approach may be particularly effective in regions like Africa, where vaccine hesitancy and misinformation are prevalent. By addressing the specific concerns of individuals and providing them with accurate and targeted information, policymakers may be able to overcome some of the barriers to vaccination and protect more people from the threat of COVID-19.
